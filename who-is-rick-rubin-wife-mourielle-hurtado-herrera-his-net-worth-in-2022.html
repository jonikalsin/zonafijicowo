Rick Rubin's Early Life

Rick Rubin was born on March 10, 1963, in Long Beach, New York and grew up near Lido Beach.

His father's name is Michael Rubin, and his mother is Linda Rubin. His father was a shoe wholesaler, while his mother was a housewife. He attended Long Beach High School, where he befriended the director of the audiovisual department Steve Freeman. Steve taught him about guitar playing and songwriting.

After learning to play the guitar, he became a band member with his childhood friends Marc Greenhut, Carlos Ferreiro, and Joey Ferrante. They performed at the garage and school shows until their teacher assisted them in creating a punk band called The Pricks. 

After completing two songs, they became famous after being thrown off the stage at CBGB. They got into a fight with the audience, started by his friends at the band. They were told to behave like that to close the show and create a buzz.

His father drove from Nassau County, New York, to Manhattan wearing his Long Beach auxiliary police uniform as he sought to shut down the event, even though he had no power in New York City.

Rick Rubin's Career At American Recordings

Rick Rubin initially gave his new label the name 'Def American Recordings.'

When Rubin learned that the term "def" had been added to the official dictionary in 1993, he organized a funeral for it, complete with a coffin, a burial, famous mourners, and a eulogy by The Reverend Al Sharpton.

Def American transformed into American Recordings after this incident. In response to this, he said that when def went from street lingo mainstream, it killed its purpose.

The renamed label's first significant project was American Recordings by Johnny Cash which was released in 1994. It consisted of a record that included six cover songs and some new material written by others at the request of Rubin for Cash.

The album was a hit both critically and commercially. It became a factor in the revival of Cash's career after a fallow period. The same formula was repeated five times with Cash's Five albums. All of the albums became a hit, and one of them earned a Grammy for Best Male Country Vocal Performance.

Additionally, Joe Strummer's final two songs, "Redemption Song" and "Long Shadow," which Cash never recorded but which Strummer penned for him to do so, were produced by him.

Both tunes were included in Strummer's final album, Streetcore, which was made public after his passing. Cash's posthumous box set, Unearthed, included a rendition of 'Redemption Song' that Rubin and Strummer jointly recorded.

The band has sold more than 80 million albums Around the world. The majority of which were Rubin-produced releases. Additionally, Rubin has employed other Chili Peppers musicians in different endeavours. The band revealed in late 2014 that Danger Mouse would produce their eleventh studio album, ending their 24-year partnership with Rubin. 

Rick Rubin Years At Columbia Records

Rick Rubin became the co-president of Columbia Records in May 2007.

The 2007 album Minutes to Midnight by Linkin Park was co-produced by Mike Sim Shinoda. Later, the band's albums A Thousand Suns from 2010 and Living Things from June 2012 were co-produced by him and Shinoda. 

He was recognized with the Grammy Award for Producer of the Year Due to his collaboration with the Dixie Chicks and other artists. He won the award again in 2008 for collaborating with Neil Diamond, Jakob Dylan, Weezer, and Metallica.

He won the Grammy Award for the Album of the Year in 2007 and 2012 for Dixie Chicks' Taking the Long Way and Adele's 21, respectively. He resigned from Columbia Records in 2012 and revived American Recordings after striking a deal with Republic Records.

He released the albums La Future and The Carpenter under his new deal. La Future belonged to ZZ Top, whereas The Carpenter belonged to The Avett Brothers.

In 2012, he tried to work with Crosby, Stills & Nash to create a cover album, but the brief sessions failed. According to Graham Nash, the meetings were 'not a terrific experience' and irritable. 

He inked a contract with Endeavor Content in July 2021 to expand his home studio, Shangri-La Recording Studios.

Rick Rubin Production Style

Rubin's most recognizable style as a producer was The "stripped-down" sound, which eliminates production features like string sections, backup vocals, and reverb in favour of raw instruments and naked vocals.

But by the 2000s, Rubin's was well known to have these components, as The Washington Post noted: "As the song builds to a climax and Diamond's commanding baritone soars over a rising string arrangement, Rubin leans back, perhaps stunned by the song's emotional impact."

He is excellent with sound and arrangements, and he is tremendous with musicians, according to music journalist Dan Charnas, who served as vice president of A&R and marketing at Rubin's American Recordings label in the 1990s.

He demonstrates how to improve it, and no one else can elicit more sincere and engaging performances from others. According to Natalie Maines of The Chicks, he has the talent and the patience to let the music be discovered, not created, who has complimented his production techniques. According to producer Dr Dre, Rubin is the most awesome producer anyone could hope to be.
